Visualization by Tom Kunicki, Charles Maalouf, Janak Mayer & Katherine Shelley

The purpose of this visualization is to walk the viewer through the complicated world of Presidential campaign financing in the United States. All data in this visualization comes from the Federal Election Commision (www.fec.gov). Each section delves into a different area of campaign financing, with interactive features that allow for filtering and summarizing in order to enhance understanding.

Outside Groups

While outside groups have always played outsized role in American politics, their influence has increased dramatically since 2010. In that year, the United States Supreme Court ruled in Citizens United v. FEC that the first amendment prohibited the government from restricting independent political expenditures - that is contributions to and expenditures made by outside groups not coordinating their activities with a political candidate.

Contributions to candidates and their committees are strictly regulated and capped at a relatively low level - in the 2015-2016 election cycle, an individual can give a maximum of $2,700 to any candidate committee, or a maximum of $5,000 to a traditional Political Action Committee (PAC). The aftermath of Citizens United, however was to see a proliferation of "independent-expenditure-only Political Action Committees", also known as SuperPACs. Contributions to these groups are entirely uncapped, enabling the wealthiest Americans to have an outized voice in recent elections.

To date, since the era of SuperPAC's began, SuperPAC fundraising has been dominated by the Republican side of politics. While Obama in 2012 had a highly funded SuperPAC, Priorities USA Action, it served almost entirely as a vehicle to fund attacks against Mitt Romney, rather than by which to promote Obama's candidacy. Pro-democratic outside groups are still relatively more likely to be small-donor traditional PACs run by organizations like trade unions, rather than SuperPACs.
